The Great Barrier Reef

About

At first you may think it's not beatiful, but when you look closer - you understand. The Great Barrier Reef is a unique place, a home, for unique species which has to be protected.

The Great Barrier Reef is one of the seven wonders of the natural world, and pulling away from it, and viewing it from a greater distance, you can understand why. It is larger than the Great Wall of China and the only living thing on earth visible from space.

The Great Barrier Reef is the world's largest coral reef system, composed of over 2.900 individual reefs and 900 islands stretching for over 2.300 kilometres over an area of approximately 344.4 km2.

Discover the GBR

Snorkelling

Snorkelling is one of the most popular activities at the Great Barrier Reef. There are different reefs with some featuring an abundant amount of plant life, with colours bursting out from everywhere. Other ones feature unique animals, with bales of sea turtles or groups of dugongs.

ATTENTION! The Great Barrier Reef is dying. Corals reefs globally occupy less than 1% of the ocean, but support 25% of all marine life. 30% of all reefs are estimated to be severely damaged, and close to 60% may be lost by 2030.

Both hard and soft reef-building corals can only exist within a limited range of conditions, needing light and an optimum temperature and salinity range.

Other species living on the reef, such as clams and parrotfish eat corals, contributing to bioerosion, so there is a natural reef cycle of production and destruction.

    The threats to coral reefs include:
  • increased acidity - the pH is dropping due to the amount of CO2 absorbed from the atmosphere into the ocean;
  • overfishing or damage to habitats by the fishing industry;
  • increased sedimentation and eutrophication due to human industrial activity including agricultural and commercial pollution and deforestation;
  • littering, pollution and habitat destruction from tourism

Most of the threats above are caused directly or indirectly by human activity.

Even corals that recover are harmed, since periods of stress can hurt their ability to reproduce. After mass bleaching in 2016 and 2017, large parts of the Great Barrier Reef lost half of their live corals. Then another bleaching event hit in 2020.

Reefs around the world are experiencing similar climate-related damage. A worldwide assessment found that between 2009 and 2019, 14 percent of the world's corals died.
