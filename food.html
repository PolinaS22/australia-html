Aussie cuisine

Australian meals are more diverse than ever, influenced by aisles of inexpensive ingredients, a platter of cultures and a menu of resurgent interest in food. Blessed with a wide variety of climates, from the warm tropical north to the cooler southern regions, there’s little Australia can’t grow or produce.

Ask an Australian how they spent their weekend, and there’s a good chance they spent at least some of it in a café – there’s nothing many of us like more than whiling away a few hours with friends or family over brunch.

Few dishes locals call their own:

The lamington is the "National Cake of Australia."

A celebration of Australia Day is incomplete without lamingtons on the table. Cake is made of a sponge cake with chocolate and lots of desiccated coconut, a layer of jam or cream is put in the middle of two lamington halves. Today, you will easily find lamingtons in cafes and bakeries, often paired with coffee or tea.

Get it: My little cupcake, 62 Ben Boyd Rd, Neutral Bay NSW 2089, Australia

Pavlova is a famous Australian dessert.

Dessert is said to be named after a Russian ballerina Anna Pavlova who visited Australia in 1926 and 1929. It is made of a meringue with a crispy crust and a soft inner layer. Topped with whipped cream and fresh fruits, widely available in restaurants and food shops at any time of the year. Whilst New Zealand claims that the pavlova originated from them, there’s a popular Australian legend that says this dessert was created by a chef from Perth’s Hotel Esplanade.

Get it: Hopetoun Tea Rooms, The Block Arcade, 1-2/282 Collins Street, Melbourne VIC 3000, Australia

Anzac biscuits

A delicious combination of butter, oats, golden syrup and coconut, it's no wonder these biscuits are an Aussie classic!

Get it: Bindoon Bakehaus & Café, 27c Binda Place, Bindoon, Western Australia, 6502

Australia’s’ first take-away food was the meat pie.

Australians do not claim to be the origin of meat pies, but one thing is for sure: it is the country’s first take-away food, long before fast food chains were a thing. Aussies are into meat pies so much that they even have an Official Great Aussie Pie Competition, a national event held since 1990. Meanwhile, the oldest surviving pie cart in Australia is the Harry’s Café de Wheels located in Sydney.

Get it: Harry’s Cafe de Wheels, Cowper Wharf Roadway, Woolloomooloo NSW 2011, Australia

Barramundi

The barramundi is one of Australia's most popular foodfishes. Barramundi gets its name from the Aboriginal language meaning “large-scaled river fish”. Barramundi is a healthy type of fish. It's nutritious sea bass with great texture, taste, and nutrition.

Get it: Little Beach Boathouse, 4 Victoria Parade, Nelson Bay NSW 2315, Australia

Hamburger with beetroot

You can eat burgers all over the world, but nothing is more Australian than slinging a piece of beetroot on top a pattie made with Aussie beef. The flavors of the beets mesh perfectly with the pineapple and cut through the robust and rich flavors of the beef and bacon.

Get it:Burger Project, World Square, Upstairs Level 1, 644 George St, Sydney NSW 2000, Australia

Ginger has been an essential part of Australian cuisine since the 1800s.

There are many Resipes with ginger, one of them is Prawn and Snow Pea Steamed Dumplings.

Get it: Dumpling Alley, Shop 1113/425 Burwood Hwy, Wantirna South VIC 3152, Australia

Grilled kangaroo

Kangaroo meat was legalised for human consumption in South Australia in 1980. In New South Wales, Queensland, and Victoria it could only be sold as pet food until 1993. Due to its low fat content, kangaroo meat cannot be cooked in the same way as other red meats, and is typically either slow cooked or quickly stir-fried.

Get it: Blackbird Cafe, Wheat Road Balcony Level 1, Cockle Bay Wharf Darling Harbour, NSW 2000, Australia

Vegemite on toast

Happy little vegemites enjoy it for breakfast, lunch and tea, so goes the much-played anthem. This is as Australian food as it gets. Easily to be enjoyed: spread the toast as soon as possible and apply an even spread of vege.

Tim Tam

The much-loved chocolate biscuit is made up of two layers of chocolate-malted biscuit, separated by a light chocolate filling and coated in melted chocolate. Arnott’s (which produces Tim Tams) say that around 35 million packs are sold each year.

Get it: you can now find them in supermarkets around the world.
